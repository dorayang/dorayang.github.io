Cave Rescue Communication Module
2022 - 2023
The wireless cave communication module was my 5th year Capstone project. The project, sponsored by Dr. Carl Michal of UBC Physics and Astronomy, was inspired by the Thai soccer team that was trapped in and rescued from the flooded Tham Luang cave in 2018.

Dr. Michal’s vision for the project was to create a network of floating communication devices that could transmit text messages through the air and through water in parallel as a redundancy measure. My team narrowed the scope of the project to develop the underwater wireless text messaging system. By reverse engineering a fish finder we used an acoustic transducer to successfully send and receive text messages at 6 characters per second up to 100 meters apart. Focusing our design on robustness, we reduced our message error rate to 2%.

Transmit and receive firmware
Project Report
Schematics for the transmit power electronics and the receive side analog to digital pulse detector created in KiCAD. On the transmit side, the flyback converter acts as a DC-DC converter boosting a 5V input up to 100V to act as a source for the H-Bridge. The H-Bridge configuration allows for a 200Vpp 200kHz square wave to be applied across the transducer so that it can vibrate and transmit the messages.

CAN Accesories PCB
2022
Controller Area Network (CANBus) is a vehicle communication standard that allows microcontrollers to communicate without having to rely on a host computer. The UBC Supermileage team uses CANBus to control the function of accessories in their urban concept vehicle. This includes sounding the horn, actuating the window wipers and turning on the headlights.

The CAN Accessory boards consist of a microcontroller and a series of connectors that allow a modular plug and play functionality for different accessories. CANBus allows a series of boards to be connected throughout the vehicle and they can all talk to eachother.

Dynamometer
2023
UBC Supermileage is a student team that designs super fuel efficient cars. The Dynamometer project was a collaboration between UBC Supermileage’s powertrain and electrical teams. A Dynamometer is a device that measures the torque and rpm of a motor. Our goal was to build a custom Dyno to run our competition vehicles on so that we could simulate competition track conditions.

I designed the electronics for the controller. This involved testing and integrating force and optical sensors for torque and RPM measurements, designing an AC to DC convertor to use a microcontroller’s PWM signal to control a brake module, a rotary encoder for manual control of the brake, integrating an LCD screen to display live data and designing the PCB for the controller.

Ligo Coating Tester
2021 - 2022
This project was my 4th year capstone project. The project sponsored by the UBC Quantum Matter Institute with Dr. Kirsty Gardner, Dr. Matthew Mitchell and Dr. Jeff Young involved devising methods to study the mechanical loss associated with coatings used in LIGO’s gravitational wave interferometers. Their work involved depositing a coating on mechanical disks and then vibrating them and measuring the amount of time it took for the vibrations to stop giving them information about the loss associated with each coating.

Our team devised a system for measuring the coating loss, redesigning the mechanical disks to better suit LIGO’s system and devising a signal processing method to isolate measurements from noise.

Machine Learning Parking Simulation
2021
This project was implemented for UBC’s ENPH 353 course competition which was a time trial to drive through a race course and identify a series of license plates. My teammate and I’s implementation utilizes a mix of machine learning and computer vision methods written using the Keras, TensorsFlow and OpenCV2 libraries in Python. Our solution was split into vehicle navigation, obstacle detection and license plate identification.

Doublie Slit Experiment Numerical Simulation
2022
This project consisted of a series of MATLAB simulations to study numerical/finite difference methods such as the Crank-Nicholson and Alternating Direction Implicit Methods for solving partial differential equations, specifically the 1D and 2D Schrödinger equation. In quantum mechanics, Wave-particle duality states that every particle or quantum entity has both particle and wave-like properties. The non-dimensionalized Schrödinger equation is given by: The Schrödinger equation allows us to determine the wave function ψ of a system which relates the location of a particle at a given point in space(x, y, z) at a particular time (t) to the wave amplitude. The amplitude of the wave function has no real significance. However the square of the wave function, is the probability of finding an electron at a given point.

Finite difference methods are a class of numerical techniques that convert/discretize ordinary and partial differential equations into a system of linear equations that can be solved using matrix algebra. In the simulations I implemented the Crank-Nicholson and Alternating Direction Implicit Methods and studied how the solutions to the wave equation changed with respect to different potentials (initial and boundary conditions).

We observe that the wave function forms a diffraction pattern with the double slit just like in the experiment performed by Dr. Thomas Young. With the barrier we see that the wave function does not have enough energy to overcome the potential thus the probability of it ending inside the confines of the barrier is zero. Next, we consider the wave function starting inside the well which is analogous to the particle in a box. In a classical system a particle trapped in a box is no more likely to be found at one position over another. In a quantum system the particle may only occupy certain energy levels (related to the geometry and potential of the box) meaning it may be more likely to occupy certain positions in the box leading to the repeating eigenmode distributions in the animation.

Recycling Robot
2020
When the world shut down due to the covid pandemic in 2020 I spent my summer in my basement designing and building this robot. My team worked on this remotely as part of the ENPH 253 robot summer competition. The objective was to design a robot that could pick up 6 empty pop cans and dump them in a recycling bin in under 60 seconds.
With the pandemic we had to think outside of the box and build the robot out of readily available materials instead of machining parts and I also had to do all of my circuit debugging with a handheld single channel oscilloscope which was certainly a fun challenge.

The design my team came up with consisted of a ultrasonic distance sensor to detect the presence of cans, an IR sensor for homing navigation to the recycling bin, an H-bridge to drive the motors, a scooping arm and holding bucket that were actuated by servo motors. I soldered all of the circuits and assembled the rest of the robot using materials such as corrugated plastic, zip ties and hot glue.
